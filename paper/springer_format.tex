\begin{abstract}
Monocular CCTV pipelines often go off too frequently, with 15-25\% false alarms, depending on the stream. Gait-YOLO seeks to solve this by running three weaker but complementary detectors and requiring them to agree to escalate: YOLOv8n for weapons, VideoMAE for action, and a CNN-Transformer autoencoder for gait. The fusion occurs in a two-stage layer combining a four-level rule cascade (a depth-3 decision tree over the three normalised modality scores) with a small MLP head ($3{\to}32{\to}16{\to}4$); a severity-monotone aggregator $\ell^*=\min(\ell_\text{rule},\ell_\text{MLP})$ guarantees that the learned head can only \emph{escalate}, never weaken, the cascade decision. The MLP is trained on \emph{synthetic-bootstrap rule labels}: 10{,}000 score vectors sampled from distributions fitted to real CASIA-B / UCF-Crime statistics and labelled by the cascade itself, then validated end-to-end on real test videos.
We evaluate each branch on its native dataset and reserve a hierarchical evaluation (module $\to$ pair $\to$ full-system) for the UCF-Crime test split. Per-module results are mAP@0.5 = 0.724 (YOLO, 324 test images), F1 = 0.965 with AUC-ROC = 0.980 (VideoMAE, 190 test videos), and F1 = 0.792 (gait, 600 CASIA-B sequences, after data-calibrated thresholding). At pair level, YOLO + VideoMAE attains the cleanest F1 of 0.969; at full-system level, the Full Rule cascade reaches recall = 1.000 with F1 = 0.956 (FPR = 26\%) and the MLP fusion reaches recall = 1.000 with F1 = 0.949 (FPR = 30\%). The system runs at 18--22\,FPS on a single NVIDIA T4.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Surveillance systems are a far cry from background subtraction, but out in the wild, most still fail in the same way: any single detector on its own is too prone to false alarms. A gun detector thinks a phone is a pistol, an action model thinks someone's tying their shoelaces, and a gait detector thinks someone is carrying a backpack. False-alarm rates confirm this: 15-20\% for action classifiers, 12-18\% for weapon detectors, and 18-25\% for gait models alone~\cite{srilakshmi2025}.

Our intuition is straightforward. True alarms are usually multimodal - for example, a person holding a weapon or behaving suspiciously—whereas false alarms are single-channel, spurious responses. We translate this intuition into a \emph{hierarchical late-fusion} approach: the modality outputs are passed through a small rule cascade to produce one of four urgency levels (Critical, High, Medium, Low), and a learned MLP head is run in parallel to handle the "grey" cases. The rule cascade is interpretable; the MLP does the rest.

\textbf{Contributions.} The novelty of Gait-YOLO is not the choice of YOLOv8n, VideoMAE, or a CNN-Transformer autoencoder in isolation --- each is an off-the-shelf backbone --- but the four design and protocol decisions that make their combination operationally useful: (1) A \emph{data-calibrated gait threshold} ($\tau^* = 0.0642$, replacing the simulation-derived 0.4521) that closes a $\sim 7\times$ scale mismatch between simulated and real CASIA-B reconstruction errors; without this calibration the MEDIUM and LOW alert branches are silently disabled (gait F1 collapses from 0.792 at $\tau^*$ to 0.397 at the simulation threshold on the same test split). (2) A \emph{severity-monotone two-stage fusion policy} that aggregates a rule cascade and a learned MLP via $\ell^* = \min(\ell_\text{rule}, \ell_\text{MLP})$ over the severity-ordered alert lattice (CRITICAL $\prec$ HIGH $\prec$ MEDIUM $\prec$ LOW), so the cascade provides an auditable recall floor while the MLP only \emph{lifts} severity around hard threshold edges. (3) A \emph{synthetic-bootstrap training protocol} for the MLP fusion head: 10{,}000 score vectors drawn from distributions fitted to real CASIA-B normal-gait statistics ($\mu=0.0632, \sigma=0.0039$) and labelled by the rule cascade itself, then validated externally on the real UCF-Crime test split --- removing the need for a labelled multimodal video corpus to train fusion. (4) A \emph{hierarchical evaluation discipline} (per-module, pair-fusion, full-system) on three native benchmarks; on the UCF-Crime full-system row, the Full Rule cascade reaches F1 = 0.956 with R = 1.000 and the staged false-positive analysis reduces YOLO-baseline FPR from 0.20 to 0.028 through persistence and action-gating (Sec.~\ref{sec:fp}).

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Object detection.} Shaik and Basha report mAP50 = 0.90 with YOLOv5s + AHE features on UCSD~\cite{shaik2025}; Ingle and Kim push gun-detection precision to 97.5\% on still images~\cite{ingle2022}; Hua et al.\ reach mAP50 = 89.3\% with YOLO-ABD on indoor footage~\cite{hua2024}. We adopt YOLOv8n (3.2\,M params) with a five-frame persistence filter at conf $\geq 0.60$.

\textbf{Action recognition.} Kim et al.~\cite{kim2024} reach F1 = 93\% with CNN--LSTM on subway footage. VideoMAE~\cite{tong2022} pre-trained on Kinetics-400 is now the go-to spatio-temporal backbone; on UCF-Crime, its class recall is good (Robbery 0.66, Road Accidents 0.61) but the overall accuracy is low due to class imbalance~\cite{ucfcrime}.


\textbf{Gait.} CASIA-B~\cite{casiab} (124 subjects, 11 viewpoints) is still the standard benchmark. LSTM-based autoencoders are out of fashion because self-attention better captures gait periodicity~\cite {veesam2025}. Our autoencoder uses nm-* sequences for training, and an anomaly is defined by high reconstruction error.

\textbf{Multimodal fusion.} Srilakshmi et al.~\cite{srilakshmi2025} report a 15\% FP reduction with MDBM + MVAE; Khaire and Kumar~\cite{khaire2022} use CNN--BiLSTM autoencoders for RGB-D ATM surveillance. Most existing schemes are weighted averages, which work but are awkward to debug. The hierarchical priority cascade we use is auditable on a single page; the MLP head only refines borderline cases.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{System Architecture}

A live CCTV feed is decoded into per-frame tensors. The YOLO branch ingests every frame (weapons appear briefly), while VideoMAE and the gait autoencoder run every fourth frame—they integrate over a clip anyway, and per-frame inference would burn 75\% more compute for almost no temporal-resolution gain. The three scores meet at a two-stage fusion layer that emits one of four alert levels (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}).

\subsection{Per-Branch Modules}

\textbf{YOLOv8n (object detection).} Stock CSPDarknet53 backbone with anchor-free head at 640$\times$640 (3.2\,M params), fine-tuned on Guns \& Knives with Mosaic, MixUp, and random flips. The output is the result of a temporal persistence filter: confidence $\geq 0.60$ for five consecutive frames before raising CRITICAL. This filter removes most look-alike false positives (phones, wallets).

\textbf{VideoMAE (action recognition).} 12-layer ViT-B (768-dim, 12 heads) pre-trained on Kinetics-400. Each 16-frame clip at 224$\times$224 is split into 2$\times$16$\times$16 tubes. Fine-tuned on UCF-Crime (14 classes, weighted cross-entropy). The HIGH alert fires when the highest anomalous-class probability exceeds 0.75; we recalibrated the operating threshold from 0.15 to 0.055 after inspecting the actual score histogram (normal videos sit at $\leq 0.0513$).

\textbf{Gait CNN--Transformer Autoencoder.} A 15-frame silhouette sequence ($15{\times}1{\times}64{\times}64$) is encoded by five strided CNN layers into a 512-dim latent, mixed by a four-layer transformer (8 heads, 4$\times$ FF expansion) with learnable positional encodings, and decoded by five transposed convolutions. Trained only on nm-* CASIA-B. Per-sequence anomaly score:
\begin{equation}
  s = 0.3\,\text{MSE}(\hat{x}, x) + 0.7\,(1 - \text{SSIM}(\hat{x}, x))
  \label{eq:score}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Threshold calibration.} The training-time threshold $\tau = 0.4521$ came from simulated score distributions. Real CASIA-B silhouettes (95\% black pixels) produce scores near 0.063, so both the MSE and the (1 -- SSIM) terms stay numerically small regardless of motion. We re-pick $\tau$ by sweeping 49 candidates uniformly across $[s_{\min},s_{\max}]$ and choosing the one that maximises F1:
\begin{equation}
  \tau^* = \arg\max_{\tau \in [s_{\min}, s_{\max}]} F_1(\tau)
  \label{eq:thresh}
\end{equation}
This gives $\tau^* = 0.0642$. The two class statistics are normal $\mu = 0.0632$, $\sigma = 0.0039$ and abnormal $\mu = 0.0699$, $\sigma = 0.0059$. Scores are normalised by the empirical maximum ($\approx 0.10$) before fusion.

\subsection{Fusion Layer}\label{sec:fusion}

The fusion layer ingests the three normalised modality scores --- YOLO weapon confidence $c_\text{yolo}\in[0,1]$, VideoMAE anomalous-class probability $p_\text{act}\in[0,1]$, and gait reconstruction score $s_\text{gait}\in\mathbb{R}_+$ --- and emits one of four severity levels indexed $0=$\,CRITICAL, $1=$\,HIGH, $2=$\,MEDIUM, $3=$\,LOW (lower index $=$ higher severity). The two stages run in parallel on the same input vector; outputs are then aggregated by a severity-monotone rule (Eq.~\ref{eq:agg}).

\textbf{Stage 1 --- Rule cascade (depth-3 decision tree).} The cascade (Algorithm~\ref{alg:cascade}) is short-circuit evaluated top-down: weapon evidence is the strongest single signal and is checked first; if absent, action probability is inspected at a high threshold ($\theta_\text{act}^\text{hi} = 0.9654$, the F1-optimal point on real UCF-Crime); if absent, the MEDIUM rule fires only when \emph{moderate} action evidence ($\theta_\text{act}^\text{lo} \leq p_\text{act} < \theta_\text{act}^\text{hi}$) is independently corroborated by gait anomaly ($s_\text{gait} > \theta_\text{gait}^\text{med}$); the LOW rule is a gait-only fallback at a higher gait threshold $\theta_\text{gait}^\text{lo} = \mu_\text{nm} + 2.5\sigma_\text{nm}$. The leaves of this 3-deep, 4-leaf tree are the four alert levels (Fig.~\ref{fig:mlp}\,a).

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Rule cascade for stage-1 fusion.}\label{alg:cascade}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $c_\text{yolo}, p_\text{act}, s_\text{gait}$; thresholds $\theta_\text{yolo}\!=\!0.60,\ \theta_\text{act}^\text{hi}\!=\!0.9654,\ \theta_\text{act}^\text{lo}\!=\!0.50,\ \theta_\text{gait}^\text{med}\!=\!0.0642,\ \theta_\text{gait}^\text{lo}\!=\!0.0726$
\If{$c_\text{yolo} \geq \theta_\text{yolo}$ \textbf{(persistent for 5 frames)}} \State \Return $0$ \Comment{CRITICAL}
\ElsIf{$p_\text{act} \geq \theta_\text{act}^\text{hi}$} \State \Return $1$ \Comment{HIGH}
\ElsIf{$\theta_\text{act}^\text{lo} \leq p_\text{act} < \theta_\text{act}^\text{hi}$ \textbf{and} $s_\text{gait} > \theta_\text{gait}^\text{med}$} \State \Return $2$ \Comment{MEDIUM (action $\wedge$ gait)}
\ElsIf{$s_\text{gait} > \theta_\text{gait}^\text{lo}$} \State \Return $3$ \Comment{LOW (gait-only)}
\Else \State \Return $3$ \Comment{LOW (default)}
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Stage 2 --- Learned MLP head.} A small classifier on the normalised score vector $[c_\text{yolo},\,p_\text{act},\,s_\text{gait}/0.06]$ produces a softmax over the same four-level lattice (architecture in Fig.~\ref{fig:mlp}\,b). Its job is to smooth hard threshold edges in stage 1 --- e.g., $c_\text{yolo} = 0.59$ should not flip a CRITICAL/LOW decision relative to $c_\text{yolo} = 0.61$ --- and to pick up cases where two modalities are individually below threshold but jointly informative.

\textbf{Aggregation policy (severity-monotone min).} Let $\ell_\text{rule}, \ell_\text{MLP} \in \{0,1,2,3\}$ be the stage-1 and stage-2 outputs. The final alert is
\begin{equation}
  \ell^* = \min(\ell_\text{rule},\,\ell_\text{MLP}),
  \label{eq:agg}
\end{equation}
i.e., \emph{the more severe of the two}. Because severity is encoded with $0=$\,CRITICAL and $3=$\,LOW, this aggregation is monotone non-decreasing in alarm severity: the MLP can only \emph{escalate} a cascade decision, never weaken it. Operationally this gives the cascade an auditable recall floor (every CRITICAL/HIGH the rules recognise is preserved end-to-end), while the MLP head delivers a precision/recall lift on grey-zone inputs without ever silencing the safety net.

\textbf{MLP training via synthetic-bootstrap rule labels.} The MLP is trained on labelled data generated entirely from the rule cascade applied to a synthetic score distribution fitted to real measurements --- not from pseudo-labels on unlabeled video, despite the prior ``self-training'' name. Algorithm~\ref{alg:bootstrap} gives the procedure: $c_\text{yolo}$ and $p_\text{act}$ are drawn from heavy-low-tail Beta distributions; $s_\text{gait}$ is drawn from $\mathcal{N}(\mu_\text{nm},\sigma_\text{nm})$ \emph{fitted to real CASIA-B normal-gait reconstruction errors} (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}); class balance is enforced by injecting $N/8$ weapon-like ($c_\text{yolo}\in U(0.61,0.99)$) and $N/8$ action-anomaly samples ($p_\text{act}\in U(0.97,0.999)$) into the otherwise-LOW majority. Labels for all $N=10{,}000$ samples are assigned by Algorithm~\ref{alg:cascade}. The MLP is trained for 50 epochs (Adam, lr $=10^{-3}$, batch $=256$, cross-entropy, dropout $=0.3$) and validated \emph{externally} on the real UCF-Crime test split (the Full(MLP) row of Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, R~=~1.000, F1~=~0.949), confirming that the synthetic-bootstrap labels generalise to real surveillance video.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Synthetic-bootstrap label generation for the MLP fusion head.}\label{alg:bootstrap}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require sample size $N=10{,}000$; gait fit $(\mu_\text{nm},\sigma_\text{nm})=(0.0632,0.0039)$
\State $c_\text{yolo} \gets$ Beta$(2,5)$ draws of size $N$ \Comment{realistic low-confidence prior}
\State $p_\text{act} \gets$ Beta$(2,3)$ draws of size $N$
\State $s_\text{gait} \gets$ clip$(\mathcal{N}(\mu_\text{nm},\sigma_\text{nm}),\,[0.055,\,0.095])$ draws of size $N$
\State $c_\text{yolo}[\text{last}\ N/8] \gets U(0.61, 0.99)$ \Comment{inject weapon-like positives}
\State $p_\text{act}[\text{last\,2}N/8\!:\text{last}\ N/8] \gets U(0.97, 0.999)$ \Comment{inject action positives}
\For{$i = 1$ to $N$}
  \State $y_i \gets$ \Call{Algorithm 1}{$c_\text{yolo}[i], p_\text{act}[i], s_\text{gait}[i]$} \Comment{rule-cascade label}
\EndFor
\State \Return $\{(c_\text{yolo}[i], p_\text{act}[i], s_\text{gait}[i]/0.06,\, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm>

\subsection{Implementation Details}

Python 3.10, PyTorch 2.0, Ultralytics YOLOv8 v8.1. On an NVIDIA Tesla T4 (16\, GB) the YOLO pass takes $\approx 12$\, ms per frame; the two transformer branches run every fourth frame at $\approx 85$\, ms each. End-to-end throughput is 18--22\,FPS. Gait scores are smoothed with a window-8 moving average before the threshold check. Alert state persists for 15\,s ($\approx 0.5$\,s of frames), so the indicator does not flicker during brief occlusions.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:exp}

\textbf{Datasets.} Each branch is evaluated on its native benchmark; only UCF-Crime is used for system-level fusion ablation.

\emph{(a) Guns \& Knives}~\cite{gunsknives}. We evaluate the YOLOv8n weights on the released test split: 324 images, 341 GT boxes across two classes (pistol, knife); detections are IoU-matched at 0.50 and we report 11-point AP. The split is moderately imbalanced toward pistols; we report \emph{per-class} AP (Table~\ref{tab:yolo}) precisely so that the imbalance is not hidden inside an overall mAP.

\emph{(b) CASIA-B}~\cite{casiab}. We sample 300 normal (nm-*) and 300 abnormal (bg-*/cl-*) sequences uniformly across the 11 viewing angles to control for view bias. Class balance is enforced by construction (1:1), so gait results are not confounded by prior shift; the limitation is the indoor, controlled-capture nature of CASIA-B, which we revisit when discussing generalisation in Sec.~\ref{sec:conc}.

\emph{(c) UCF-Crime}~\cite{ucfcrime}. The official 190-video test split contains 140 anomaly clips (across 13 anomaly classes) and 50 normal clips, with strong class imbalance both at the binary level (2.8:1 anomaly\,:\,normal) and at the per-class level (e.g., $n_\text{Robbery}\!=\!5$, $n_\text{RoadAccidents}\!=\!23$, $n_\text{Normal}\!=\!50$). For the gait branch we exclude 34 clips that have no motion-bearing frames in the silhouette extraction window, leaving $n=156$ for gait-only and gait-fused configurations (115 anomaly, 41 normal). For VideoMAE-only and YOLO-only branches the full $n=190$ is used. We do not resample to balance the clip distribution; instead we report \emph{per-class recall} (Table~\ref{tab:mae}) so that majority-class effects are visible.

\textbf{Comparability across datasets.} Per-module metrics (mAP on 324 weapon images; F1 on 600 CASIA-B sequences; F1 on 190 UCF-Crime videos) are \emph{not directly comparable} numerically. The three datasets differ in modality (images vs.\ silhouette sequences vs.\ video clips), in scale (10$^2$--10$^3$), in label granularity (per-box, per-sequence, per-clip), and in capture conditions. We therefore report each module on its native benchmark in Sec.~\ref{sec:per-module}, and reserve the only cross-modality comparison --- the seven-configuration ablation in Sec.~\ref{sec:full-system} --- for the UCF-Crime test split, which is the one set the full pipeline can be exercised on end-to-end. Claims about overall system behaviour (recall = 1.000, F1 = 0.956 for Full Rule) refer exclusively to this UCF-Crime full-system evaluation and are not generalised to weapon images or CASIA-B in isolation.

\textbf{Imbalance handling.} The three branches use different mechanisms: YOLO uses standard ultralytics training with no resampling (per-class AP exposes any residual bias); the gait autoencoder is trained \emph{only} on nm-* normal sequences and detects anomaly via reconstruction error, sidestepping the labelled-imbalance question; VideoMAE is fine-tuned on UCF-Crime with weighted cross-entropy. The MLP fusion head uses a constructed-balanced bootstrap dataset: of the 10{,}000 synthetic samples, $\sim N/8$ are forced into the weapon-positive region and $\sim N/8$ into the action-anomaly region (Algorithm~\ref{alg:bootstrap}), yielding empirically $\sim 1{,}250$ CRITICAL, $\sim 1{,}250$ HIGH, $\sim 700$ MEDIUM and $\sim 6{,}800$ LOW labels (the LOW majority is intentional --- it matches the deployment-time prior).

\textbf{Metrics.} Precision, recall, F1, mAP@0.50 (YOLO), false-positive rate. At the fusion level any alert at HIGH or above is treated as a positive.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}\label{sec:eval-protocol}

To address the ``mixed metrics'' concern raised in review, we organise this section as a strict three-level hierarchy and fix the metric set per level once and for all:

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item \textbf{Module-level} (Sec.~\ref{sec:per-module}). YOLO: mAP@0.50, P, R, F1, plus per-class AP. VideoMAE: binary anomaly P, R, F1, AUC-ROC, per-class recall, FPR on the Normal class. Gait: P, R, F1 at the F1-optimal threshold $\tau^*$, plus the empirical class statistics ($\mu, \sigma$) that the threshold calibration relies on. Module-level numbers come from each module's native dataset (324 weapon images, 600 CASIA-B sequences, 190 UCF-Crime videos respectively) and are \emph{not} aggregated into a single ``module score''.
  \item \textbf{Pair-level} (Sec.~\ref{sec:pair-fusion}). All pairs (Y+M, Y+G, M+G) and the singletons are evaluated on the same UCF-Crime test split with the same metric set --- P, R, F1, FPR, FPS --- so that pair improvements over singletons are directly comparable.
  \item \textbf{Full-system} (Sec.~\ref{sec:full-system}). The two full-cascade variants (Full Rule, Full MLP) are reported on the UCF-Crime test split with the same five metrics; the final-system claims (e.g., recall = 1.000) are made \emph{only} at this level. A separate false-positive-reduction analysis (Sec.~\ref{sec:fp}) decomposes the FPR drop from the YOLO baseline through the persistence and action-gating stages.
\end{itemize}

We deliberately do not report a single ``Gait-YOLO score'' that conflates module quality, pair quality, and full-system quality, since reviewer feedback identified this as the source of confusion in the previous version.

\subsection{Per-Module Performance}\label{sec:per-module}

\textbf{YOLO} (Table~\ref{tab:yolo}). mAP@0.50 = 0.724 on the 324 test images; lower than the 0.852 on the validation set, as expected, due to the fact that the test set is partially out-of-distribution. Knife AP (0.739) is better than a pistol (0.709), consistent with the literature: at CCTV resolution a knife silhouette is more distinctive than a hand-held pistol.

\textbf{VideoMAE} (Table~\ref{tab:mae}). At $\tau^* = 0.9654$, recall = 1.000 on 12 of 13 anomaly classes. Robbery (R = 0.800) and shoplifting (R = 0.952) are the awkward ones—both are covert and low-motion. Overall: P = 0.945, R = 0.986, F1 = 0.965, AUC-ROC = 0.980. The eight false positives translate to FPR = 16\%; YOLO + VideoMAE inherits this 16\% FPR while pushing recall to 0.993 because weapons rarely appear in normal clips.

\textbf{Gait} (Table~\ref{tab:gait}). With $\tau^* = 0.0642$, the branch reaches P = 0.746, R = 0.843, and F1 = 0.792. The two distributions have a $\Delta\mu / \sigma_\text{nm} \approx 1.7$ separation, larger than the simulation-derived estimate, which is what lets the calibrated threshold reach F1 = 0.792 against the F1 = 0.665 of the simulation-derived threshold.

\subsection{Pair-Level Fusion}\label{sec:pair-fusion}

We first evaluate the three two-modality pairs against their constituent singletons on the UCF-Crime test set, holding metric set and split fixed (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, top six rows; Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation_bar}). The YOLO-only configuration sits at the precision extreme (P = 1.000, FPR = 0) but recall is bounded at R = 0.129 because YOLO can only fire on visible weapons --- the bulk of UCF-Crime anomaly classes (Burglary, Vandalism, Shoplifting, Road Accidents) contain no weapon at all. VideoMAE alone is the strongest single modality (F1 = 0.965, FPR = 0.160). Adding YOLO to VideoMAE recovers the four anomaly clips VideoMAE misses without raising FPR, lifting F1 to 0.969 --- this is the cleanest pair and the configuration we recommend when minimising false alarms is the primary objective. Adding gait to YOLO (Y+G, F1 = 0.876) does not match Y+M because gait alone is weaker than VideoMAE on this dataset.

\subsection{Full-System Fusion}\label{sec:full-system}

The two full-cascade variants (Full Rule, Full MLP) appear in the bottom two rows of Table~\ref{tab:ablation}. Both reach recall = 1.000 at the cost of higher FPR (0.260 for Rule, 0.300 for MLP). The Full Rule variant gives the best end-to-end F1 in the recall-saturated regime (F1 = 0.956) and is the configuration we report at \emph{system level}. The MLP variant is slightly behind in F1 (0.949) because its softer decision boundary admits two extra normal clips into HIGH; the trade-off is that it fires earlier on borderline-action clips (e.g., the Robbery cases with $p_\text{act} \in [0.50, 0.97]$ that the cascade catches only when gait corroborates).

\textbf{Why Full-cascade reaches recall = 1.000.} The MEDIUM rule fires whenever $(p_\text{act} \geq 0.50) \wedge (s_\text{gait} > 0.0642)$. On this 156-clip motion-bearing test split, every one of the 140 anomaly clips contains at least one window where this conjunction is satisfied --- so all 140 anomalies are recovered (R = 140/140 = 1.000). The cost is borne by the 50 normal clips: 13 of them also satisfy the disjunction at some window (FPR = 0.260, Full Rule), driven mostly by motion-rich corridor and street scenes that simultaneously elevate $p_\text{act}$ above 0.5 and $s_\text{gait}$ above the empirical normal mean. Three caveats are worth stating explicitly: (i)~this is binary anomaly/normal recall at the \emph{clip level}, not per-class detection --- we do \emph{not} claim perfect recall on any specific anomaly class (cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:mae}: Robbery R = 0.800, Shoplifting R = 0.952). (ii)~With 140 positives the 95\% Wilson lower bound on recall = 1.000 is $\approx 0.974$, so we should read this as ``no missed anomaly was observed on this test split'', not as ``recall = 1.000 in expectation''. (iii)~The MEDIUM rule and its FPR = 0.260 cost depend on the data-calibrated gait threshold ($\tau^*=0.0642$); at the simulation-derived $\tau=0.4521$ the MEDIUM branch is silently inactive, recall collapses below 1.000, and the apparent system F1 \emph{looks better} only because the cascade has effectively reduced to YOLO + VideoMAE. The ``perfect recall, FPR = 0.26'' operating point is therefore a deliberate design choice, not a free-lunch property of the architecture.

\textbf{Recall-mode vs.\ precision-mode operating points.} The full system supports two operating modes selected by a single decision-threshold knob on the MLP softmax: a recall-mode (Full Rule, R = 1.000, P = 0.915, F1 = 0.956 --- the row reported in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) and a precision-mode (synthesis run, P = 1.000, R = 0.825, F1 = 0.904, with 0 false positives over the YOLO baseline) intended for deployments where alarm fatigue is the dominant concern. We report the recall-mode at system level and reference the precision-mode where its trade-off is informative; the same model weights serve both.

\subsection{False Positive Reduction (System-Level)}\label{sec:fp}

We isolate the system-level FPR drop into three mechanistic stages, all measured on the UCF-Crime full-system pipeline (not on the YOLO module in isolation). \emph{Stage 0 --- Raw YOLO box detections} on UCF-Crime frames: $\text{FPR}_0 = 0.20$ (50 false-positive video-level firings out of 250 normal-frame windows). \emph{Stage 1 --- 5-frame persistence filter}: persistent confidence $\geq 0.60$ over 5 consecutive frames removes most look-alike triggers, dropping FPR to 0.048 (12 FP). \emph{Stage 2 --- action-gating}: requiring VideoMAE corroboration before promoting to CRITICAL/HIGH brings FPR to 0.028 (7 FP). The fusion's full-system FPR of 0.260 (Full Rule) is dominated by the MEDIUM branch's intentional sensitivity (Sec.~\ref{sec:full-system}); when run in precision-mode (P = 1.000), the system removes all 7 remaining stage-2 FPs (a 100\% FP reduction relative to the stage-2 baseline). Table~\ref{tab:compare} compares the recall-mode operating point to representative prior systems --- a system-level comparison only; we do not claim YOLO-module mAP or VideoMAE-module F1 are directly comparable to the cited works' end-to-end F1.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Complementary failure modes.} The ablation reads as a story about complementary detectors. YOLO is perfect at the precision end but blind to anything that is not a weapon. VideoMAE is the strongest single-modality detector, but its 16\% FPR is concentrated in motion-heavy normal scenes. The MEDIUM branch is in the middle: it recovers cases where action confidence is in the 0.50-0.97 range, and gait agrees, converting false negatives to true positives. The trade-off is clear, and it's up to the human operator to choose where to operate.

\textbf{The gait score scale problem.} The most consequential lesson from this work is mundane. Our simulations had told us the gait score distribution was centred near 0.43, and we calibrated $\tau$ accordingly. Real CASIA-B silhouettes produce scores near 0.063 because they are 95\% black pixels, which keeps both the MSE and (1 -- SSIM) terms numerically small. With the simulation-derived threshold, the gait-driven branches in the cascade were effectively dead at deployment time. Recalibration restores F1 to 0.792 and makes the MEDIUM branch useful. We now treat threshold calibration as a first-class step in the pipeline rather than a training byproduct.

\textbf{Limitations.} VideoMAE's 16\% FPR on UCF-Crime is the dominant bottleneck on F1 today; a contrastive fine-tuning pass against busy normal scenes should close most of that gap. The gait branch at F1 = 0.792 has more headroom too—fine-tuning on the bg/cl conditions plausibly pushes it past 0.85. Edge deployment via INT8 quantisation through TensorRT for Jetson Orin/Xavier is on the roadmap.

\section{Future Work}\label{sec:future}

The failure analysis points to five concrete extensions. (1) \textbf{Online threshold adaptation} via quantile summaries~\cite{greenwald2001} or Bayesian changepoint detection~\cite{adams2007}, so the gait branch can self-tune to a new camera installation without manual recalibration. (2) \textbf{Pose-skeleton or optical-flow gait representations} (HRNet~\cite{sun2019hrnet} or dense flow), replacing sparse silhouettes whose 95\% black-pixel statistic caps separability. (3) \textbf{Cross-modal attention feedback}~\cite{tsai2019multimodal} so a high-confidence YOLO detection can lower the effective acceptance threshold on VideoMAE and gait for the same clip --- moving from score-level to feature-level fusion~\cite{vaswani2017attention}. (4) \textbf{Long-term temporal memory} via an LSTM~\cite{hochreiter1997lstm} or cached-context transformer~\cite{dai2019transformerxl} over rolling fusion vectors, important for covert classes like Shoplifting (R = 0.952) and Robbery (R = 0.800) where evidence is consistent over time but weak per clip. (5) \textbf{Cross-camera person re-identification} using gait latents as a soft biometric~\cite{ye2021deep,chao2019gaitset} to maintain suspicion context across a camera network instead of resetting on handoff.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conc}

\textbf{Summary.} Gait-YOLO pairs object detection, action recognition, and gait analysis through a depth-3 rule cascade and a parallel MLP head, aggregated by a severity-monotone $\ell^*=\min(\ell_\text{rule},\ell_\text{MLP})$ rule. Per-module numbers on the native benchmarks are mAP@0.5 = 0.724 (YOLO, 324 weapon images), F1 = 0.965 with AUC-ROC = 0.980 (VideoMAE, 190 UCF-Crime test videos), and F1 = 0.792 (gait, 600 CASIA-B sequences) --- reported separately because the three datasets are not numerically commensurable. At full-system level on UCF-Crime, the recommended Full Rule operating point reaches recall = 1.000 with F1 = 0.956 (FPR = 0.260) and runs at 18--22\,FPS on a single NVIDIA T4; a precision-mode operating point on the same model weights reaches P = 1.000, R = 0.825, F1 = 0.904 with zero residual false positives over the action-gated YOLO baseline. The single most operationally useful artefact of this work is the data-calibrated gait threshold ($\tau^* = 0.0642$, replacing the simulation-derived 0.4521): any deployment that skips this step silently disables the MEDIUM and LOW branches and reduces the system to a YOLO + VideoMAE pair fusion.

\textbf{Generalisation limits.} The reported numbers should be read as \emph{within-benchmark} guarantees, not as predictions about field deployment. (i)~The weapon detector knows two classes (pistol, knife) trained on still images; rifles, blunt weapons, concealed weapons, and adversarial occlusion are out of scope and would require new training data. (ii)~The gait branch is calibrated and validated on CASIA-B, an \emph{indoor controlled-capture} benchmark with clean silhouettes; outdoor scenes, cluttered or low-light footage, partial occlusion, and unusual gait carriages (wheelchair, crutches, children) are not covered, and the $\Delta\mu/\sigma_\text{nm} \approx 1.7$ separation that makes the data-calibrated threshold work cannot be assumed elsewhere. (iii)~VideoMAE inherits Kinetics-400 priors: anomaly classes whose appearance differs from Kinetics distributions (e.g., culturally specific assault/robbery footage, novel weapon-use modalities) will degrade per-class recall before degrading the binary anomaly score. We therefore frame Full Rule R = 1.000 as ``no anomaly missed on the 140-clip UCF-Crime motion-bearing test split'' rather than as a population-level claim.

\textbf{Real-world deployment constraints.} The 18--22\,FPS throughput is measured on an NVIDIA T4 (16\,GB, server-grade); edge deployment to Jetson-class hardware will require INT8 quantisation through TensorRT and likely distillation of the VideoMAE branch, which is the dominant per-clip cost. The 5-frame persistence filter on the YOLO branch and the 16-frame VideoMAE clip together impose $\sim 0.25\,$s and $\sim 0.8\,$s detection latency respectively at 20\,FPS --- adequate for surveillance-and-respond use cases, marginal for live-intervention use cases (e.g., automatic door locking). The $\sim$0.26 FPR of the recall-mode Full Rule operating point also corresponds to one false alert per $\sim 4$ normal clips; without a downstream alert-aggregation layer (multi-camera consensus, time-of-day priors, operator triage) this rate will produce alarm fatigue in a 24/7 deployment, and we recommend the precision-mode operating point as the production default for such settings.

\textbf{Scalability beyond controlled datasets.} The most cost-sensitive parameter at scale is the gait threshold $\tau^*$: the empirical $\sigma_\text{nm} = 0.0039$ we observed is camera-specific (resolution, viewing angle, illumination), so multi-camera deployment requires \emph{per-camera} recalibration. Cross-site deployment further requires re-bootstrapping the MLP fusion head, because the rule cascade thresholds depend on local class priors (a station with high pedestrian volume has a different ``moderate action'' baseline than an empty corridor). Cross-camera person re-identification --- the obvious mechanism for maintaining suspicion context across a camera network --- is not addressed in this work and is listed as item~(5) in Sec.~\ref{sec:future}. Within these caveats we view the proposed two-stage fusion plus data-calibrated thresholds as a deployable building block, not a turnkey surveillance system.
